Basic Arithmetic
Numbers · Operations · BODMAS
▼
Natural Numbers
Counting numbers starting from 1: 1, 2, 3, 4, … They do NOT include 0 or negative numbers.
Whole Numbers
All natural numbers PLUS zero: 0, 1, 2, 3, … Every natural number is a whole number.
Integers
All positive, negative whole numbers including zero: …−3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3…
BODMAS Rule — Order of Operations
B → Brackets first ( ), { }, [ ] O → Orders (powers, square roots) D → Division ÷ M → Multiplication × A → Addition + S → Subtraction −
📝 Solved Example
Evaluate: 3 + 6 × (5 + 4) ÷ 3 − 7
Step 1 — Brackets: (5+4) = 9
Step 2 — Multiply: 6 × 9 = 54
Step 3 — Divide: 54 ÷ 3 = 18
Step 4 — Left to right: 3 + 18 − 7 = 14
✅ Answer = 14
  • Place value of a digit = digit × its positional value (ones, tens, hundreds…)
  • Face value of a digit is always the digit itself regardless of position
  • Commutative Property: a+b = b+a and a×b = b×a
  • Associative Property: (a+b)+c = a+(b+c)
  • Distributive Property: a×(b+c) = a×b + a×c

Fractions & Decimals
Proper · Improper · Mixed · Conversions
▼
Fraction
A number of the form p/q where q ≠ 0. The top number is the numerator; the bottom is the denominator.
Proper: numerator < denominator Improper: numerator ≥ denominator Mixed: whole number + fraction
Fraction Operations
Add/Sub: a/b ± c/d = (ad ± bc) / bd Multiply: a/b × c/d = ac / bd Divide: a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c = ad/bc
Conversion
Fraction → Decimal: divide numerator by denominator Decimal → Fraction: write over power of 10, simplify Example: 0.75 = 75/100 = 3/4 Example: 1/3 = 0.333... (recurring)
📝 Solved Example
Simplify: 2/3 + 3/4
LCM of 3 and 4 = 12
2/3 = 8/12  ·  3/4 = 9/12
Sum = 8/12 + 9/12 = 17/12
✅ Answer = 1 and 5/12
  • To compare fractions, convert to same denominator using LCM
  • Reciprocal of p/q is q/p; dividing by a fraction = multiplying by its reciprocal
  • Terminating decimals: denominator has only factors 2 and 5 (e.g. 1/4 = 0.25)
  • Recurring decimals: denominator has other prime factors (e.g. 1/3 = 0.333…)

Percentages & Ratios
Ratio · Proportion · % Applications
▼
Percentage Formulas
Percentage = (Part / Whole) × 100 Part = (Percentage × Whole) / 100 % Increase = [(New − Old) / Old] × 100 % Decrease = [(Old − New) / Old] × 100
Ratio & Proportion
Ratio a : b means a/b Proportion: a:b :: c:d → ad = bc (cross product) Fourth Proportional: if a:b = c:x → x = bc/a Mean Proportional of a, b = √(ab)
📝 Solved Example
A score went from 80 to 100. Find % increase.
% Increase = [(100−80)/80] × 100 = [20/80] × 100 = 25%
✅ 25% increase
📝 Solved Example
If A:B = 3:5 and B:C = 4:7, find A:B:C
Make B common — LCM(5,4) = 20
A:B = 12:20  ·  B:C = 20:35
✅ A:B:C = 12:20:35
  • x% of y = y% of x — useful calculation trick
  • If price increases r%, qty must decrease by r/(100+r)×100% to keep expenditure same
  • Compound ratio of a:b and c:d is ac:bd
  • Duplicate ratio of a:b is a²:b² — sub-duplicate ratio is √a:√b

Profit & Loss
CP · SP · Discount · Marked Price
▼
Core Formulas
Profit = SP − CP Loss = CP − SP Profit% = (Profit / CP) × 100 SP = CP × (100 + Profit%) / 100 CP = SP × 100 / (100 + Profit%)
Discount & Marked Price
Discount = Marked Price − Selling Price Discount% = (Discount / MP) × 100 SP = MP × (100 − Discount%) / 100 Profit% = [(MP × (100−D%) / 100) − CP] / CP × 100
📝 Solved Example
A man buys a cycle for ₹1400 and sells it for ₹1680. Find profit%.
Profit = 1680 − 1400 = ₹280
Profit% = (280/1400) × 100 = 20%
✅ Profit% = 20%
📝 Solved Example
Shopkeeper marks price 25% above CP, gives 10% discount. Profit%?
Let CP = 100. MP = 125.
SP = 125 × 90/100 = 112.50
Profit = 12.50
✅ Profit% = 12.5%
  • Two items sold at same SP — one at x% profit, other at x% loss → always a net LOSS of (x²/100)%
  • Successive discounts a% and b% = single discount of (a + b − ab/100)%
  • If CP of x items = SP of y items → Profit% = [(x−y)/y] × 100
  • Dishonest trader uses false weights: Gain% = [Error/(True value − Error)] × 100

Simple & Compound Interest
SI · CI · Difference Formula
▼
Simple Interest
SI = (P × R × T) / 100 Amount (A) = P + SI P = 100 × SI / (R × T) R = 100 × SI / (P × T) T = 100 × SI / (P × R)
Compound Interest
A = P × (1 + R/100)ⁿ CI = A − P Half-yearly: A = P × (1 + R/200)^(2n) Quarterly: A = P × (1 + R/400)^(4n)
CI − SI Shortcuts
For 2 years: CI − SI = P × (R/100)² For 3 years: CI − SI = P × (R/100)² × (R/100 + 3)
📝 Solved Example
Find CI on ₹8000 at 10% p.a. for 2 years (compounded annually).
A = 8000 × (1.1)² = 8000 × 1.21 = ₹9680
CI = 9680 − 8000 = ₹1680
✅ CI = ₹1680
  • SI is always the same every year; CI grows each year (interest on interest)
  • If annual CI differs in 2 consecutive years by ₹X → X = CI₁ × R/100
  • Population formula (growth): P_n = P₀ × (1 + r/100)ⁿ — same as CI formula

Time, Speed & Distance
Trains · Relative Speed · Boats & Streams
▼
Basic Formulas
Speed = Distance / Time Distance = Speed × Time Time = Distance / Speed km/hr → m/s: multiply by 5/18 m/s → km/hr: multiply by 18/5
Trains
Cross a pole/person: Time = Length of train / Speed Cross a platform: Time = (Train + Platform length) / Speed Same direction: Relative speed = |S₁ − S₂| Opposite direction: Relative speed = S₁ + S₂
Boats & Streams
Downstream speed = u + v (u=boat speed, v=stream speed) Upstream speed = u − v Boat speed u = (Downstream + Upstream) / 2 Stream speed v = (Downstream − Upstream) / 2
📝 Solved Example
A 200m train crosses a 100m platform in 15 seconds. Find speed in km/hr.
Total distance = 200 + 100 = 300 m
Speed = 300/15 = 20 m/s = 20 × 18/5 = 72 km/hr
✅ Speed = 72 km/hr
  • Average speed for equal distances = 2S₁S₂/(S₁+S₂) — NOT simple average
  • If A is n times faster than B, A takes 1/n times the time
  • Two people start towards each other — they meet after: time = D/(S₁+S₂)

Time & Work
Pipes · Cisterns · Efficiency
▼
Core Concept
If A finishes work in n days → A's 1-day work = 1/n If A+B work together: 1/d = 1/A + 1/B d = AB / (A + B) 3 people: d = ABC / (AB+BC+CA)
Pipes & Cisterns
Inlet pipe fills in A hrs → rate = +1/A per hour Outlet pipe empties in B hrs → rate = −1/B per hour Net rate (A inlet, B outlet) = 1/A − 1/B Time to fill with both open = AB/(B−A) when B>A
MDH Formula
M₁ × D₁ × H₁ = M₂ × D₂ × H₂ (Men × Days × Hours = constant for same work) Useful when workers or hours change
📝 Solved Example
A can do work in 12 days, B in 18 days. They work together 4 days, then A leaves. How many more days does B need?
Together rate = 1/12 + 1/18 = 5/36 per day
Work in 4 days = 4 × 5/36 = 20/36 = 5/9
Remaining = 1 − 5/9 = 4/9
B's time = (4/9) × 18 = 8 days
✅ B needs 8 more days
  • Work is inversely proportional to time for the same efficiency
  • If A is x times as efficient as B → A takes 1/x of B's time
  • Negative work = destruction/emptying; subtract from total work

Averages
Mean · Weighted Average · Age Problems
▼
Average Formula
Average = Sum of all values / Number of values Sum = Average × n New avg when 1 value added = (Old sum + new) / (n+1) New avg when 1 value removed = (Old sum − removed) / (n−1)
Useful Shortcuts
Average of 1 to n = (n+1)/2 Average of first n odd numbers = n Average of first n even numbers = n + 1 Average of consecutive numbers = (first + last)/2
📝 Solved Example
Average of 5 numbers is 27. If 35 is replaced by 55, find new average.
Old sum = 27 × 5 = 135
New sum = 135 − 35 + 55 = 155
New average = 155/5 = 31
✅ New Average = 31
  • If wrong reading x is replaced by y → change in average = (y−x)/n
  • Weighted average = Σ(weight × value) / Σ(weights)
  • When a person leaves/joins a group, compare their age with group average to find new average

HCF & LCM
Prime Factorisation · Euclidean Algorithm
▼
HCF — Highest Common Factor
The largest number that divides all given numbers exactly. Also called GCD (Greatest Common Divisor).
LCM — Least Common Multiple
The smallest positive number that is divisible by all given numbers without remainder.
Key Relationship
HCF × LCM = Product of two numbers (a × b) LCM is always a multiple of HCF HCF always divides LCM HCF of fractions = HCF(numerators) / LCM(denominators) LCM of fractions = LCM(numerators) / HCF(denominators)
📝 Solved Example
Find HCF and LCM of 36 and 48.
36 = 2² × 3²  ·  48 = 2⁴ × 3
HCF = 2² × 3 = 12
LCM = 2⁴ × 3² = 144
Check: 12 × 144 = 1728 = 36 × 48 ✓
✅ HCF = 12, LCM = 144
  • Euclidean method: HCF(a,b) = HCF(b, a mod b) — repeat until remainder = 0
  • Bells ringing together: they ring simultaneously after LCM of their intervals
  • Largest tile to cover a floor without cutting: HCF of dimensions

Squares, Cubes & Roots
Perfect Squares · √ · ∛ · Algebraic Identities
▼
Quick Reference Table
1²=1 2²=4 3²=9 4²=16 5²=25 6²=36 7²=49 8²=64 9²=81 10²=100 11²=121 12²=144 13²=169 14²=196 15²=225 1³=1 2³=8 3³=27 4³=64 5³=125 6³=216 7³=343
Key Algebraic Identities
(a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b² (a−b)² = a² − 2ab + b² (a+b)(a−b) = a² − b² (a+b)³ = a³ + 3a²b + 3ab² + b³ a³ + b³ = (a+b)(a² − ab + b²) a³ − b³ = (a−b)(a² + ab + b²)
📝 Solved Example
Find √1764 without a calculator.
1764 = 4 × 441 = 4 × 21² = (2 × 21)²
√1764 = 42
✅ √1764 = 42
  • A perfect square always ends in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, 9 — NEVER 2, 3, 7, 8
  • Square of numbers ending in 5: (a5)² = a(a+1) followed by 25. E.g. 35² = 1225
  • Sum of first n odd numbers = n² (e.g. 1+3+5+7 = 16 = 4²)

Ancient India
Indus Valley · Vedic · Maurya · Gupta
▼
Indus Valley Civilisation (3300–1300 BCE)
One of the world's earliest urban civilisations. Major cities: Mohenjo-daro and Harappa. Known for advanced drainage systems, town planning, standardised weights, and a script still undeciphered.
Vedic Period (1500–600 BCE)
Period of the Rig Veda (oldest of 4 Vedas). Aryan settlements; caste system emerged; Sanskrit language developed. Four Vedas: Rig, Sama, Yajur, Atharva.
Maurya Empire (322–185 BCE)
Founded by Chandragupta Maurya (guided by Chanakya/Kautilya) Greatest ruler: Ashoka — embraced Buddhism after Kalinga War (261 BCE) Arthashastra: treatise on statecraft by Kautilya Capital: Pataliputra (modern Patna, Bihar)
Gupta Empire (320–550 CE) — "Golden Age of India"
Founded by Sri Gupta; greatest ruler: Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya) Aryabhata — discovered zero, calculated value of π ≈ 3.14 Kalidasa — wrote Shakuntala (Sanskrit drama) Nalanda University — world's first residential university
  • Buddhism founded by Gautama Buddha (563 BCE, Lumbini, Nepal); attained enlightenment at Bodh Gaya
  • Jainism founded by Mahavira (599 BCE); promoted Ahimsa (non-violence) and Anekantavada
  • Alexander the Great invaded India 326 BCE; defeated by Porus at Battle of Hydaspes
  • Ashoka's Rock Edicts spread across India; written in Brahmi script — spread Buddhism to world

Medieval India
Delhi Sultanate · Mughals · Vijayanagara
▼
Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) — 5 Dynasties
① Slave/Mamluk: Qutub-ud-din Aibak (built Qutub Minar) ② Khilji: Alauddin Khilji (market reforms; repelled Mongols) ③ Tughlaq: Muhammad bin Tughlaq (moved capital to Daulatabad) ④ Sayyid: Khizr Khan ⑤ Lodi: Ibrahim Lodi (defeated by Babur in 1526)
Mughal Empire (1526–1857)
Babur — founded 1526; defeated Ibrahim Lodi at 1st Battle of Panipat Humayun — lost/regained throne; died falling from library steps Akbar the Great — Din-i-Ilahi; Navratnas; Mansabdari system; secular Shah Jahan — Taj Mahal, Red Fort, Jama Masjid Aurangzeb — last powerful Mughal; imposed Jizya tax
Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)
Founded by Harihara and Bukka Raya. Greatest ruler: Krishnadevaraya — poet (Amuktamalyada), warrior, patron of arts. Capital: Hampi — now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
  • Battle of Talikota (1565) — Coalition of Deccan Sultans defeated Vijayanagara; Hampi destroyed
  • Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj founded Maratha Empire; expert in guerrilla (ganimi kava) warfare
  • Sikhism founded by Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469); 10 Gurus; Guru Granth Sahib — holy scripture
  • Bhakti Movement: Sant Kabir, Mirabai, Tukaram, Ramananda — promoted devotion over caste/ritual

Indian Polity & Constitution
Fundamental Rights · Parliament · Judiciary
▼
Constitution — Key Facts
Father: Dr. B.R. Ambedkar (Chairman, Drafting Committee) Adopted: November 26, 1949 Effective: January 26, 1950 Preamble: "Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic" Originally: 395 Articles, 8 Schedules, 22 Parts Currently: 470+ Articles, 12 Schedules, 25 Parts Longest written constitution in the world
6 Fundamental Rights (Part III, Art. 12–35)
① Right to Equality (Art. 14–18) — no discrimination ② Right to Freedom (Art. 19–22) — speech, movement, etc. ③ Right Against Exploitation (Art. 23–24) — no child labour ④ Right to Freedom of Religion (Art. 25–28) ⑤ Cultural & Educational Rights (Art. 29–30) — minorities ⑥ Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art. 32) — "Heart & Soul"
Parliament Structure
Lok Sabha (Lower House): 543 elected + 2 nominated; max 552 Rajya Sabha (Upper House): 238 elected + 12 nominated; max 250 President: Head of State; elected by electoral college (MPs + MLAs) Prime Minister: Head of Govt; leads Cabinet; advises President Supreme Court: Chief Justice + up to 33 judges; Art. 124
  • Directive Principles (Part IV) — non-justiciable but fundamental to governance; guide lawmakers
  • 11 Fundamental Duties added by 42nd Amendment (1976) under Art. 51A
  • 3 types of Emergencies: National (Art. 352), State (Art. 356 — President's Rule), Financial (Art. 360)
  • CAG — Comptroller and Auditor General; guardian of public purse; Art. 148
  • Election Commission — independent constitutional body; conducts free and fair elections

Physics
Motion · Force · Energy · Light · Sound
▼
Newton's Laws of Motion
1st Law (Inertia): body at rest stays at rest unless an external force acts 2nd Law: F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration) Unit: Newton (N) 3rd Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction
Work, Energy & Power
Work = Force × Distance × cos θ Unit: Joule (J) KE (Kinetic Energy) = ½ mv² PE (Potential Energy) = mgh Power = Work / Time Unit: Watt (W) 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J (unit on electricity bill)
Light & Sound
Speed of light: 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum Speed of sound in air: ~343 m/s at 20°C Concave mirror — converging; used in torches, vehicle headlights, telescopes Convex mirror — diverging; used in rear-view mirrors, security mirrors Total Internal Reflection → optical fibre, mirage, diamonds sparkle
  • Archimedes' Principle: upthrust (buoyancy) = weight of fluid displaced by the object
  • Pascal's Law: pressure applied to enclosed fluid transmits equally in all directions (basis of hydraulics)
  • Decibel (dB) — unit of sound intensity; threshold of pain = 120 dB; normal conversation ≈ 60 dB
  • Ultrasonic waves: frequency > 20,000 Hz; used in SONAR, ultrasound scanning, cleaning
  • Electromagnetic spectrum (low to high freq): Radio → Micro → IR → Visible → UV → X-ray → Gamma
